Abstract
University admission offices in Pakistan face an acute operational challenge during annual
enrollment seasons, when hundreds of voice and digital queries arrive simultaneously from
prospective students and their families, the vast majority of whom communicate in Urdu rather
than English. This report presentsMahir Connect: Advanced Elucidation Support in Local
Language, a real-time Urdu-language voice AI web application developed for the front desk
of FAST-NUCES Peshawar. The system enables any visitor to speak a query in Urdu, receive
a spoken answer synthesised from a curated domain-specific knowledge base, and view a si-
multaneous text transcript. The architecture integrates OpenAI Whisper for automatic speech
recognition [1], GPT-4o-mini with Retrieval-Augmented Generation for knowledge-grounded
response generation [2], and a neural text-to-speech engine for natural Urdu audio output [3, 4].
A category-structured ChromaDB vector store [5] organises institution-specific information
into eleven thematic domains, and a password-protected administration panel permits autho-
rised staff to expand the knowledge base without developer involvement. Evaluation demon-
strates an average end-to-end pipeline latency of approximately 4.8 seconds, with accurate re-
sponses to all in-scope evaluation queries and appropriate fallback behaviour for out-of-scope
queries. The system substantially reduces the volume of repetitive queries handled by human
staff while improving accessibility for Urdu-speaking applicants who are currently excluded
from English-first digital information channels.

1. Introduction
Conversational artificial intelligence has undergone a period of rapid maturation, moving from
narrow rule-based dialogue systems to large-scale neural architectures capable of open-domain
conversation [8, 3]. Yet despite this progress, the deployment of conversational AI in regional-
language, domain-specific settings remains limited. The overwhelming majority of commer-
cially available voice assistants and chatbot platforms are optimised for English and a small
number of high-resource languages [9]. Communities whose primary language is Urdu, with
over 230 million speakers one of the most widely spoken languages in the world, continue to
receive inferior service or no service at all from these systems [10].
The consequences of this gap are concrete and measurable in Pakistan. In Pakistan, 42 percent
of adults cannot read in any language [6]. That is over 100 million people, locked out of
banking, healthcare, education, and government services, simply because the digital world was
built for text. University front desks face the same barrier. Applicants and parents struggle
to access basic institutional information that is buried in English portals and long queues at
administrative offices. For a student travelling from a rural district to inquire about admission
eligibility, a telephone call or a physical visit to the front desk is not a minor inconvenience; it
is a significant financial and logistical burden [7].
The annual admission season at FAST-NUCES Peshawar concentrates this pressure acutely.
Hundreds of queries arrive daily through email, telephone, and in person, covering topics that
are largely repetitive: eligibility criteria, entrance test structure, fee payment schedules, schol-
arship availability, and hostel facilities. The administrative staff who handle these queries are
performing skilled work, but the work itself is routine and could be performed by a well-
designed automated system. The opportunity cost of redirecting staff time away from complex,
judgment-dependent tasks toward answering the same questions hundreds of times each day is
substantial [11].
Mahir Connect (meaningExpert on Callin Urdu) is a real-time voice AI web application de-
signed to address this bottleneck. It allows any visitor to speak a question in Urdu, without
typing, without navigating menus, and without waiting for a human operator, and receive an
instant spoken answer grounded in the university’s official documentation. The system is acces-
sible directly from any modern web browser without additional software installation, making it
available to users on low-cost mobile devices as well as desktop computers.
The technical architecture integrates four major components: an automatic speech recognition
engine based on OpenAI Whisper [1] for transcribing spoken Urdu, a Retrieval-Augmented
Generation pipeline [2] for grounding responses in verified institutional knowledge, a large

2. Research on Existing Products
A structured survey of existing conversational AI products was conducted across three cate-
gories: general-purpose voice assistants, university-specific chatbot deployments, and enter-
prise customer-service voice AI platforms. The goal was to identify the capabilities and limita-
tions of current solutions relative to the requirements of an Urdu-language, domain-constrained,
academic front-desk assistant.
2.1 General-Purpose Voice Assistants
Amazon Alexa, Google Assistant, and Apple Siri represent the dominant commercial voice
assistant platforms [9]. These systems are engineered for broad consumer use and support a
wide range of languages. However, their design priorities are fundamentally misaligned with
the requirements of this project. They lack the ability to answer institution-specific queries
without substantial custom integration work, and their acoustic models are not tuned for Pak-
istani phonology, regional vocabulary, or the code-switching patterns common in Pakistani
Urdu speech [10]. Their closed architectures make it impossible to constrain responses to a
specific knowledge base, which is a critical requirement for a setting where factual accuracy
is non-negotiable. A response that is plausible but incorrect about admission deadlines or fee
amounts could lead an applicant to miss an important window or make an incorrect financial
decision.
2.2 University Chatbot Platforms
Several universities internationally have deployed text-based chatbots for student services. A
notable example is Georgia Tech’s Jill Watson, built on IBM Watson’s natural language pro-
cessing infrastructure, which was designed to answer forum questions in online computer sci-
ence courses [15]. Jill Watson demonstrated that a domain-specific chatbot can achieve high
accuracy when its knowledge domain is well-defined and its training data is curated. However,
it is a text-only, English-language system, and the IBM Watson platform carries per-query
pricing that is not feasible for institutions operating on academic budgets. More broadly, the
university chatbot market has been dominated by text-based interfaces, neglecting the large
segment of applicants who are more comfortable with spoken communication in their native
language [11, 16].
Among Pakistani universities, deployments of Facebook Messenger-based and web-based chat-
bots for admission queries have been observed, but these systems operate exclusively in text
and are largely rule-based, offering limited ability to handle the natural variation in how users

2.3 Enterprise Customer-Service Voice AI Platforms
Commercial platforms including Google Dialogflow CX [19], Amazon Lex [20], and Mi-

conversational agents. These platforms offer pre-built speech recognition, intent classifica-
tion, entity extraction, and dialogue management components, and their engineering quality

base with retrieval-augmented generation requires substantial custom development. The Rasa
open-source framework [18] offers a self-hosted alternative that avoids per-request costs, but
its dialogue management model requires extensive labelled training data and does not natively
support the modern large language model approach to response generation that underpins Mahir
on Call’s conversational quality and flexibility.
2.4 Speech Recognition for Urdu
Automatic speech recognition for Urdu presents particular challenges. The language uses
Arabic script written right-to-left, and spoken Pakistani Urdu incorporates significant code-
switching with English technical vocabulary and regional Pashto and Punjabi phonological
influences [10]. OpenAI’s Whisper model [1] is trained on 680,000 hours of multilingual
web audio, including a substantial Urdu component, and is the most capable openly acces-
sible model for this language at the time of writing. Deepgram’s Nova-2 commercial ASR API
[22] offers lower latency but reduced accuracy on accented Urdu. The trade-off between ac-
curacy and latency is a central engineering tension addressed in this project’s implementation.
Relevant techniques from the broader speech and language processing literature, including the
probabilistic relevance models surveyed by Robertson and Zaragoza [23] and the evaluation
metrics formalised by Papineni et al. [24], inform the evaluation methodology applied in this
project.
2.5 Text-to-Speech for Urdu
The synthesis of natural Urdu speech from text is more constrained than recognition. Mi-
crosoft Azure Neural TTS and Amazon Polly both offer Urdu voices, but these were found
to produce robotic prosody unsuitable for a customer-facing assistant during the exploratory

2.6 Retrieval-Augmented Generation
The RAG paradigm, formalised by Lewis et al. [2], provides the theoretical foundation for
grounding large language model responses in a specific document corpus. Rather than rely-
ing on the parametric knowledge encoded during pretraining, a RAG system retrieves the most
semantically relevant passages from a vector database at inference time and injects them into
the model’s context window. The Transformer architecture underlying modern large language
models [25] enables effective use of this injected context, and models such as BERT [26]
and GPT-series models [8, 3] have demonstrated strong performance on context-conditioned
question answering tasks [27]. Vector similarity search is performed using approximate near-
est neighbour techniques [28], enabling efficient retrieval over large document corpora. The
LangChain framework [29] provides orchestration utilities connecting the embedding, retrieval,
and generation components, while ChromaDB [5] serves as the persistent vector store with
SQLite backing.

3. Project Vision
3.1 Problem Statement
In Pakistan, 42 percent of adults cannot read in any language. That is over 100 million people,
locked out of banking, healthcare, education, and government services, simply because the
digital world was built for text [6, 7]. University front desks face the same barrier. Applicants
and parents struggle to access basic institutional information that is buried in English portals
and long queues at administrative offices. For a first-generation university applicant whose
family has invested significant resources in pursuing higher education, the inability to quickly
determine eligibility, deadlines, or how to pay the admission fee is not a minor inconvenience;
it is a structural obstacle that can result in a missed opportunity and a foreclosed future.
During the annual admission season, the administrative offices of FAST-NUCES Peshawar
receive hundreds of emails and telephone calls per day from prospective students and their par-
ents. The queries are predominantly repetitive, covering a predictable set of topics: eligibility
criteria, the NU-Test entrance examination, application procedures, fee structures, scholarship
policies, and hostel availability. The volume of queries consistently exceeds the capacity of
the administrative staff to respond in a timely manner, leading to delayed responses, frustrated
applicants, and an inefficient allocation of skilled human resources to routine information re-
trieval tasks. A significant proportion of the applicant pool is Urdu speaking and is not fully
comfortable with English-language digital interfaces, further reducing their ability to access
information independently [10].
3.2 Business Opportunity
A voice-first, Urdu-language AI service embedded in the university website creates value across
multiple dimensions simultaneously. For applicants and their families, it eliminates the need
to travel to the campus, wait on hold, or compose a written query in a language in which
they may not be fluent. For administrative staff, it redirects their capacity from answering the
same ten questions hundreds of times each day toward complex, judgment-dependent cases that
genuinely require human expertise. For the institution, it reduces operational costs, improves
the quality and consistency of information delivered to applicants, and demonstrates a concrete
commitment to serving all segments of Pakistani society, not only those already comfortable
navigating English-language digital systems.
The architecture of Mahir Connect is intentionally modular. The knowledge base is the only
institution-specific component; the speech recognition pipeline, the RAG engine, the language
model, and the TTS synthesiser are all parameterised by configuration values. Extending the

3.3 Objectives
The project set out to achieve five primary objectives. The first was to develop a real-time
voice AI web application accessible from any modern browser without additional software
installation, ensuring that a mobile phone with a data connection was a sufficient device to
use the system. The second was to support Urdu language voice input and output, serving the
majority of the applicant pool in their most comfortable medium of communication [10]. The
third was to provide instant, accurate answers to admission-related queries by grounding all
responses in a RAG-based knowledge base [2] derived from official FAST-NUCES Peshawar
documentation, ensuring that no answer could be fabricated by the language model. The fourth
was to display a live transcript of the conversation for accessibility, serving users who are deaf,
hard of hearing, or who prefer to read. The fifth was to achieve an average end-to-end response
latency of under five seconds for typical queries, ensuring that the conversational rhythm felt
natural rather than mechanical.
3.4 Project Scope
The system is scoped specifically to admission-related queries at FAST-NUCES Peshawar for
the academic session 2025–2026. The knowledge base covers eleven content categories: ad-
missions procedures and eligibility, general information and portal guidance, fee and payment
structures, transfer and programme change policies, degree programmes offered, academic
policies, orientation and scheduling, hostel and accommodation, scholarships and financial aid,
campus and facilities, and faculty information. Queries outside this scope are handled by a
defined fallback response that acknowledges the limitation and, where appropriate, directs the
user to the relevant administrative contact. The system does not make case-specific decisions,
as such determinations require human judgment and access to application data.

present in the curated knowledge base cannot be answered correctly, and the system is designed

4. Software Requirement Specifications
4.1 List of Features
Mahir Connect provides voice-based and text-based interaction channels through two comple-
mentary interface components. The primary voice card allows users to record a spoken query
and receive both an audio response and a text transcript. The floating chat widget, styled in the
manner of a WhatsApp conversation panel, supports both typed text input and voice recording,
with all responses displayed as timestamped message bubbles alongside speaker buttons that
trigger on-demand audio playback. Both interfaces share a common underlying hook archi-
tecture that maintains conversation history across multiple turns within a single session. The
knowledge base powering the system is partitioned into eleven thematic categories, each stored
as a separate source in the ChromaDB vector store [5]. The administration panel, accessible at
the/adminroute of the application, provides a secure login gate, a knowledge base status ta-
ble, and a file upload facility for adding new content without requiring developer access. Audio
playback is interruptible at any point, allowing the user to begin a new query before the current
response has finished.

5. Iteration Plan
The project was developed using an iterative approach with three major iterations, each building
incrementally on the previous and delivering a testable, demonstrable system at its conclusion.
This approach was chosen to allow early validation of core technical assumptions, particu-
larly the feasibility of Urdu speech recognition and language-constrained response generation,
before committing to the full feature set.
5.1 Iteration 1: Core Voice Pipeline
The first iteration established the end-to-end voice processing pipeline and validated the se-
lected technologies for each stage. The primary deliverable was a working prototype capable
of accepting a spoken Urdu query, transcribing it, generating a response from a preliminary
knowledge base, and returning that response as synthesised audio. This iteration confirmed that
OpenAI Whisper’s multilingual model [1] provided sufficient accuracy for Pakistani-accented
Urdu, that GPT-4o-mini with RAG-injected context [2] could generate factually grounded re-
sponses without hallucinating content, and that the selected TTS engine could produce accept-
ably natural Urdu speech from Roman-script input.

6. Implementation Details
6.1 System Architecture Overview
Mahir Connect follows a client-server architecture in which a React single-page application
[14] communicates with a Python FastAPI backend [13] over HTTPS. The backend acts as
an orchestrator, invoking three external AI APIs in sequence: OpenAI Whisper for speech
recognition [1], OpenAI GPT-4o-mini for response generation [3], and ElevenLabs or Uplift AI
for speech synthesis [4, 12]. The ChromaDB vector store [5] and the LangChain orchestration
framework [29] run within the same backend process, eliminating the need for a separate vector
database server and minimising deployment complexity.
6.2 Backend Implementation
The backend is structured as a Python package under thebackend/directory. Theconfig.py
module loads all environment variables from a.envfile at startup and raises a descriptive er-
ror if any required API key is absent, preventing silent failures. Themain.pymodule defines
the FastAPI application [13] with primary endpoints for voice queries (POST /voice-query),
text queries (POST /text-query), and a family of/admin/*routes for knowledge base man-
agement, all protected by HTTP Basic Authentication. CORS is configured to permit requests
from the frontend development server.
Speech-to-Text Module
Thestt.pymodule implements thetranscribe audio(audio bytes, mimetype)func-
tion. The production implementation uses the OpenAIwhisper-1API, corresponding to the
Whisper large-v3 model [1]. Audio bytes received from the frontend are written to a temporary
file with an extension derived from the MIME type, submitted to the Whisper transcription
endpoint with automatic language detection enabled, and the temporary file is deleted after the
API call completes regardless of outcome. The detected language code is normalised to a bi-
nary Urdu-or-English classification: the codesur(Urdu),hi(Hindi), andpa(Punjabi) are all
mapped to Urdu, reflecting the phonetic overlap between these languages in Pakistani speech
[10]. A commented Deepgram Nova-2 implementation [22] is preserved in the module for ref-
erence and can be re-enabled to prioritise latency over accuracy in deployments where network
conditions make the Whisper API slower.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation Module
Therag.pymodule is the core of the system’s intelligence. At module load time, a LangChain
Chroma vector store instance [29, 5] is initialised against the persistentchroma db/directory.
Thegenerate response(query, language, conversation history)function first em-
beds the query usingtext-embedding-ada-002[30] and performs a similarity search [28],
retrieving the top five most relevant chunks. The retrieved chunks are concatenated into a con-
text string, and a GPT-4o-mini completion is requested [3] with a system prompt that instructs
the model to answer in a warm and conversational manner grounded exclusively in the retrieved
context. The conversation history from the last three exchanges is injected into the messages
array between the system prompt and the current user query, enabling the model to resolve
anaphoric references across turns [8]. Themax tokensparameter is set to 300, bounding gen-
eration latency. For Urdu queries, the model returns a JSON object containing both a Roman
Urdu display string and an Arabic script Urdu string for TTS synthesis.
Theingest text(text, source label)function splits incoming text using aRecursiveCharacterTextSplitter
with a chunk size of 500 tokens and an overlap of 50 tokens [29]. Each chunk is assigned a de-
terministic identifier computed as the MD5 hash of a string combining the source label, chunk
index, and the first 40 characters of the chunk text, making the ingestion process idempotent.
Text-to-Speech Module
Thetts.pymodule exposes thesynthesize speech(text)function. Before any API call,
thepreprocess for tts(text)function applies three sequential normalisation steps [9].
URLs are replaced with the spoken word “Link” to prevent letter-by-letter pronunciation. A
hardcoded dictionary maps known institutional abbreviations to phonetically appropriate forms.
Any remaining all-caps token of four or more characters is converted to title case by a general
fallback rule. The preprocessed text is submitted to the ElevenLabs [4] or Uplift AI [12] TTS
API, and the returned raw MP3 binary data is Base64-encoded and returned as a data URL for
browser playback.

8. Conclusions and Recommendations
8.1 Conclusions
Mahir Connect demonstrates that a production-quality, domain-specific, Urdu-language voice
AI system can be built and deployed within the resource constraints of a Final Year Project,
using modern large language model APIs [3], open-source vector database technology [5],
and a React-based frontend [14]. The system meets all five primary objectives stated in Sec-
tion 3.3. It is accessible from any modern browser without installation, supports Urdu voice
input and output, grounds all responses in a curated institutional knowledge base through
retrieval-augmented generation [2], provides a live text transcript, and achieves an average
latency of 4.8 seconds that satisfies the sub-five-second target.
More significantly, the project validates a design philosophy directly relevant to the broader
challenge of digital inclusion in Pakistan. By placing spoken Urdu at the centre of the interac-
tion model rather than treating it as an afterthought, the system serves a population segment that
is systematically excluded from text-first digital services [6, 7]. The architecture demonstrates
that the gap between English-language AI products and the needs of Urdu-speaking communi-
ties is not technically unbridgeable; it is a question of design priority. The RAG approach [2]
provides a principled mechanism for constraining the system’s outputs to verified institutional
information, addressing the hallucination risk that makes unconstrained large language models
[8] unsuitable for settings where factual accuracy carries real consequences for applicants. The
category-structured knowledge base and the administration panel together ensure that the sys-
tem remains maintainable after deployment without depending on the continued availability of
the development team.
8.2 Recommendations
The knowledge base should be reviewed and updated by administrative staff before each ad-
mission season to reflect changes in fee structures, programme offerings, scholarship criteria,
and application procedures. The administration panel makes this process accessible to non-
technical staff, but a formal annual review cycle should be established to ensure it happens
consistently. Outdated information in the knowledge base is more harmful than a gap in cover-
age, because the system will return the outdated answer with the same confidence as a correct
one.
The architecture is intentionally modular. The knowledge base is the only institution-specific
component; the speech recognition, RAG engine, language model, and TTS synthesiser mod-
ules are all parameterised by configuration values. Extending the system to other FAST cam-

set and updates to a small number of configuration parameters. A phased multi-campus rollout

An analytics module logging query topics, response latency breakdowns, and user feedback
